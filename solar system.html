Our Solar System in This Universe

Our Solar System is a small part of the vast universe, which contains many galaxies. It includes the Sun and all the objects that move around it due to gravity. Scientists believe the Solar System formed about 4.6 billion years ago from a huge cloud of gas and dust that collapsed under its own gravity. Most of the Solar System’s mass is in the Sun. The remaining objects orbit the Sun in nearly circular paths along a flat surface called the ecliptic plane. The four inner planets—Mercury, Venus, Earth, and Mars—are known as terrestrial planets because they are mainly made of rock and metal. The four outer planets are much larger and are called gas giants. Jupiter and Saturn are mostly made of hydrogen and helium. Uranus and Neptune are mainly composed of ices such as water, ammonia, and methane, and are often called “ice giants.”

➢ Structure :

The inner planets are primarily made of rock. These materials have high melting points and remained solid in the early Solar System. Jupiter and Saturn are mostly made of gases, especially hydrogen and helium, which were present in the original cloud. Ices like water, methane, ammonia, carbon dioxide, and hydrogen sulfide have lower melting points. Depending on temperature and pressure, these substances can exist as solids, liquids, or gases. In the outer regions of the Solar System, many icy objects exist beyond Neptune’s orbit. All these gases and ices together are often referred to as volatile materials.

➢ Sun :

The Sun is the central star of our Solar System and its most important part. Its mass is about 332,900 times that of Earth. The Sun produces energy through nuclear fusion in its core, releasing enormous amounts of heat and light into space. Most of the Sun’s energy is emitted as electromagnetic radiation, especially visible light, which falls within the wavelength range of approximately 400 to 700 nanometers.

➢ Inner Solar System :

The inner Solar System refers to the region that includes the terrestrial planets and asteroids. These objects are mainly composed of silicate rocks and metals. They are located closer to the Sun compared to the outer planets.

➢ Mercury :

Mercury is the nearest planet to the Sun and also the smallest planet in the Solar System (about 0.055 times the mass of Earth). It has no natural satellites. Its surface is covered with many craters and ridges. Scientists believe these features were likely formed during an early period when the planet contracted as it cooled.

➢ Venus :

Venus is almost the same size as Earth (about 0.815 Earth masses). Like Earth, it has a thick silicate mantle surrounding an iron core. It also has a dense atmosphere and signs of geological activity. Venus is the hottest planet in the Solar System, with surface temperatures above 400°C, mainly due to the strong greenhouse effect caused by gases in its atmosphere.

➢ Earth :

Earth is the largest and densest of the inner planets. It is the only planet known to have recent geological activity and the only place in the universe where life is confirmed to exist. Earth has liquid water on its surface, which makes it unique among the terrestrial planets. Plate tectonics occur on Earth, and its atmosphere is very different from the others, containing about 21% free oxygen. Earth has one natural satellite, the Moon, which is the only large moon orbiting a terrestrial planet in the Solar System.

➢ Mars :

Mars is smaller than both Earth and Venus (about 0.107 Earth masses). Its atmosphere is mainly made of carbon dioxide and has a surface pressure much lower than Earth’s (about 0.6% of Earth’s pressure). The surface of Mars features huge volcanoes such as Olympus Mons and large valleys like Valles Marineris. Geological activity may have continued until about 2 million years ago. The red appearance of Mars is due to iron oxide (rust) in its soil.

➢ Outer Solar System :

The outer region of the Solar System contains the gas giants and their many moons. Short-period comets, including those from the centaur group, also orbit in this region. Because this region is far from the Sun, solid objects there contain more volatile substances such as water, ammonia, and methane. The colder temperatures allow these compounds to remain frozen.

➢ Jupiter :

Jupiter is extremely massive—about 2.5 times the mass of all the other planets combined (318 times the mass of Earth). It is mainly composed of hydrogen and helium. Jupiter’s strong internal heat produces visible atmospheric features, including cloud bands and the Great Red Spot. It has 63 known natural satellites.

➢ Saturn :

Saturn shares several similarities with Jupiter, particularly regarding its atmospheric composition and magnetosphere. Its iconic rings are composed of countless small ice and rock particles. At the time of this record, Saturn has 62 confirmed satellites, the most famous being Titan and Enceladus.

➢ Uranus :

Uranus is the lightest of the outer planets. It is unique because it orbits the Sun on its side; its axial tilt is over 90 degrees to the ecliptic. Uranus has 27 known satellites, with the largest being: ‣ Titania ‣ Oberon ‣ Umbriel ‣ Ariel ‣ Miranda

➢ Neptune :

While slightly smaller in size than Uranus, Neptune is more massive (equivalent to about 17 Earths) and consequently more dense. It radiates more internal heat than Uranus, though not as much as Jupiter or Saturn. Neptune has 13 known satellites. Its largest moon, Triton, is geologically active and features geysers that spew liquid nitrogen.
